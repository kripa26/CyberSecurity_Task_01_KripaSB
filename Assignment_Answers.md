## Part B: Password Security Review

**1. What makes a strong password?**
A secure password needs to be lengthy, ideally over 12 characters. It must include a random assortment of symbols, numbers, and both upper and lower case letters. It should never contain easily guessable information like names, birth dates, or dictionary words.

**2. Why should passwords be unique?**
Using the same password everywhere is incredibly dangerous because if one website is hacked and your credentials leak, cybercriminals will use automated tools to try logging into your other accounts, such as your bank or email, using those exact same credentials. 

**3. What is Multi-Factor Authentication (MFA)?**
MFA is an extra layer of security for your accounts. Instead of just asking for a password (something you know), it requires a second piece of evidence to prove your identity, such as a temporary code sent to your phone or a fingerprint scan (something you have or are).

**4. What are the risks of password reuse?**
The main risk is that one compromised account can quickly lead to all your accounts being compromised. Hackers frequently buy lists of leaked passwords from dark web forums and use them to orchestrate massive "credential stuffing" attacks on popular platforms.

**Proposed Password Policy:**
- Passwords must be at least 15 characters long.
- A mix of at least three character types (uppercase, lowercase, numbers, special characters) is required.
- Passwords must be changed immediately if there is any suspicion of a compromise.
- Reusing any of the last 5 passwords is strictly forbidden.
- Multi-Factor Authentication (MFA) must be activated on all company accounts.

## Part D: Cyber Threat Research

**1. Phishing**
*   **Definition:** Tricking individuals into giving away sensitive data by sending fraudulent emails that appear to come from legitimate organizations or trusted individuals.
*   **Real-world Example:** The 2014 Sony Pictures hack began with sophisticated spear-phishing emails sent to top executives, tricking them into revealing their network credentials.
*   **Prevention Methods:** Never click on suspicious links, verify the sender's actual email address (not just the display name), and use reliable email security filters.

**2. Malware**
*   **Definition:** Any software intentionally designed to cause damage to a computer, server, client, or computer network. This includes viruses, trojans, and spyware.
*   **Real-world Example:** The ILOVEYOU virus in 2000, which infected millions of computers globally by disguising itself as a love letter in an email attachment.
*   **Prevention Methods:** Use strong antivirus software, avoid downloading files from shady or unverified websites, and keep your operating system updated.

**3. Ransomware**
*   **Definition:** Malicious software that blocks access to a computer system or encrypts its data until a sum of money (ransom) is paid to the attackers.
*   **Real-world Example:** The Colonial Pipeline attack in 2021, which disrupted fuel supplies across the eastern United States after hackers encrypted the company's billing systems.
*   **Prevention Methods:** Keep regular, offline backups of important data, update software regularly to patch known vulnerabilities, and train staff not to open suspicious attachments.

**4. Social Engineering**
*   **Definition:** The art of manipulating people so they willingly give up confidential information, exploiting human psychology (like trust or authority) rather than technical flaws.
*   **Real-world Example:** The 2013 Target data breach, where attackers used social engineering tactics to steal network credentials from a third-party HVAC vendor, ultimately compromising millions of credit cards.
*   **Prevention Methods:** Implement strict identity verification processes, provide regular security training to staff, and foster a culture of questioning unusual requests for information.

**5. Data Breach**
*   **Definition:** A security incident where confidential, protected, or sensitive data is exposed to an unauthorized person or entity.
*   **Real-world Example:** The massive Yahoo data breaches (2013-2014), which affected all 3 billion of their user accounts, exposing names, email addresses, and encrypted passwords.
*   **Prevention Methods:** Encrypt sensitive databases, implement strict access controls (Zero Trust architecture), and conduct regular security audits and penetration tests.

## Part F: Security Reflection

**1. What cybersecurity risks did you identify in your own digital habits?**
I realized that I tend to save my passwords directly in my web browser, which isn't the most secure method if my device is ever compromised by malware. Also, I often delay installing software updates on my phone because they take too long, leaving my device vulnerable to known exploits. Lastly, I've noticed I'm entirely too quick to click 'accept' on cookie pop-ups without reading what data they are actually collecting.

**2. What security improvements will you implement immediately?**
I am going to switch from using browser-based password saving to a dedicated, encrypted password manager app. I will also change my device settings to install updates automatically overnight so I don't keep postponing them. Additionally, I plan to set up an authenticator app for my primary email account instead of relying on less secure SMS codes.

**3. Why is cybersecurity important in today's world?**
In our modern digital age, almost every aspect of our lives is connected to the internet, from banking and healthcare to socializing and working remotely. This connectivity brings immense convenience but also massive vulnerability. Cybersecurity is critical because a single successful attack can result in devastating financial loss, identity theft, or the exposure of deeply private information. As hackers become more advanced, everyone must take proactive steps to defend their digital footprint and protect the systems that keep modern society functioning safely.
